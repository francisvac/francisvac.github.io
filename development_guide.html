Baby Developmental Guide by Age

This reference provides age-appropriate developmental information and activity ideas. Use it to ground your recommendations in what's actually realistic and beneficial for the baby's current stage.

Week 1 (Days 0-7)

What's happening developmentally:

  • Adjusting to life outside the womb — everything is brand new
  • Vision limited to about 20-30cm (roughly the distance to a parent's face during feeding)
  • Strong preference for high-contrast patterns (black and white)
  • Hearing is well developed — recognizes parent's voice from the womb
  • Reflexes dominate: rooting, sucking, grasping, startle (Moro), stepping
  • Sleep in short bursts (16-17 hours total, but in 2-4 hour cycles)
  • Feeding every 2-3 hours; weight loss of up to 10% in first few days is normal, should regain by day 10-14

Appropriate activities:

  • Skin-to-skin contact: The single most beneficial activity. Regulates temperature, heart rate, breathing. Both parents can do this.
  • Talking and singing: Baby already knows your voice. Narrate what you're doing, sing softly. This is early language exposure.
  • Face gazing: Hold baby at feeding distance and let them study your face. Stick out your tongue slowly — some newborns will mirror this (early imitation).
  • Gentle touch/massage: Light stroking of arms, legs, back. Helps with body awareness and bonding.
  • High-contrast visuals: Black and white cards or images held 20-30cm away. Move slowly side to side — baby may briefly track.
  • Varied sounds: Gentle music, nature sounds, household sounds at a comfortable volume. Helps auditory development.

What NOT to expect:

  • Social smiling (that's around 6-8 weeks)
  • Head control (very minimal at this stage)
  • Any kind of "play" — this is about sensory exposure and comfort

Week 2 (Days 8-14)

What's happening developmentally:

  • Becoming more alert during brief wakeful periods
  • May start to briefly focus on faces and high-contrast objects
  • Beginning to uncurl from the fetal position
  • Feeding patterns may start to become slightly more predictable
  • Umbilical cord stump typically falls off around this time
  • May show brief moments of head lifting when on chest

Appropriate activities:

  • Tummy time on chest: Place baby on your chest while you recline. This is gentler than mat tummy time and still builds neck strength. Start with 1-2 minutes.
  • Tracking practice: Hold a high-contrast object (or your face) at 20-30cm and move it slowly horizontally. Baby may follow briefly.
  • Different textures: Let baby's hands and feet touch different soft textures (cotton, silk, fleece). Keep it brief and gentle.
  • Mirror time: Hold baby near a mirror. While they don't recognize themselves, faces are fascinating to newborns.
  • Gentle rocking and swaying: Vestibular input (sense of movement) helps brain development. Rock, sway, or gently bounce while holding securely.
  • Reading aloud: It doesn't matter what you read — baby benefits from hearing the rhythms and patterns of language.

Emerging skills to watch for:

  • Brief moments of alertness and focus
  • Tiny head lifts during chest tummy time
  • Reflexive hand grasping becoming slightly more purposeful

Week 3 (Days 15-21)

What's happening developmentally:

  • Alert periods getting slightly longer (still short — maybe 30-45 minutes)
  • Starting to show preferences for certain sounds or stimuli
  • May start to show early social engagement — staring at faces longer
  • Weight should be back to or above birth weight
  • Movements becoming slightly less jerky

Appropriate activities:

  • Mat tummy time (brief): Try short sessions (1-3 minutes) on a firm surface. Place a high-contrast image in front. If baby protests, switch to chest tummy time.
  • Gentle bicycle legs: While baby is on their back, gently move legs in a cycling motion. Good for digestion and body awareness.
  • Sound localization: Make gentle sounds (rattle, soft clap, your voice) from different sides. Baby may start to turn toward the sound.
  • Carrying in different positions: Upright on shoulder, cradled in arms, facing outward with support — each gives different visual and vestibular input.
  • Baby massage: Slightly more structured than week 1. Gentle strokes on legs, arms, tummy (clockwise for digestion), back. Use baby-safe oil if desired.
  • Singing with rhythm: Add gentle bouncing or swaying to songs. Combining auditory and vestibular input.

Week 4 (Days 22-28)

What's happening developmentally:

  • Nearing the end of the "fourth trimester's" first month
  • Alert periods more distinct — baby is more "present" when awake
  • May start to show early social smiling (though true social smile usually comes at 6-8 weeks)
  • Better head control — may hold head up briefly during tummy time
  • Starting to make small cooing sounds (early vocalization)
  • Can focus on objects slightly further away

Appropriate activities:

  • Extended tummy time: Work up to 3-5 minutes if tolerated. Use different surfaces and props (rolled towel under chest for support).
  • Conversation games: When baby coos, respond as if they said something. Pause and wait. This back-and-forth teaches conversational rhythm.
  • Visual tracking (vertical): Add up-and-down movement to your tracking games, not just side to side.
  • Water sounds: Running water, gentle splashing during bath time. Combine with different water temperatures on hands/feet (warm, slightly cool).
  • Babywearing walks: If you have a carrier, take baby for walks. The movement, sounds, and changing light are rich sensory input.
  • Sibling/pet introduction activities: If relevant, supervised gentle interactions with older children or calm pets.

Month 2 (Weeks 5-8)

What's happening developmentally:

  • Social smile emerges (typically around 6 weeks) — a huge milestone
  • Increasing head control, especially during tummy time
  • Starting to bat at objects (not yet reaching purposefully)
  • Cooing and vowel sounds becoming more frequent
  • Can track objects in a wider arc
  • Sleep may start to consolidate slightly (longer stretches at night for some babies)
  • Feeding becomes more efficient

Appropriate activities:

  • Play gym / activity mat: Hanging toys within batting range. Baby starts to connect "I move my arm" with "the toy moves."
  • Longer tummy time with toys: Place interesting objects just out of reach during tummy time to encourage reaching and head lifting.
  • Face-to-face play: Exaggerated expressions, peekaboo (simple version — hands over your face), funny sounds. Baby will start to smile and vocalize in response.
  • Finger and toe games: "This little piggy," gentle finger counting. Combines touch, language, and social connection.
  • Colorful objects: Baby's color vision is developing. Introduce primary-color toys and objects alongside high-contrast ones.
  • Outdoor time: Different environments (park, garden, even just a different room) provide novel sensory input.
  • Dance together: Hold baby securely and dance to music. Great for vestibular development and bonding.

Milestones to watch for:

  • First social smile
  • Holding head up at 45 degrees during tummy time
  • Batting at toys
  • Making cooing and "ah" sounds
  • Following a moving object smoothly with eyes

Month 3 (Weeks 9-13)

What's happening developmentally:

  • Much stronger head control — may hold head steady when upright
  • Starting to push up on forearms during tummy time
  • Hands open more often (not always in fists)
  • Reaching intentionally for objects
  • Laughing may emerge
  • Increasingly social — loves interaction with people
  • May start to show early hand-eye coordination

Appropriate activities:

  • Reaching games: Hold toys within reach and let baby grasp them. Vary texture, size, and weight.
  • Supported sitting: Prop baby in a supported sit (Boppy, between your legs) for a new perspective on the world.
  • Rattle play: Place a rattle in baby's hand. They'll start to connect shaking with sound.
  • Mirror play (close up): Baby may start to smile at their reflection, which is wonderful social practice.
  • Sensory bottles: Fill clear bottles with water and glitter, beads, or colorful items. Shake gently for visual tracking.
  • Reading board books: Point to pictures, use animated voices. Baby won't understand words but is absorbing language patterns.
  • Floor play on back: Hang toys above for reaching and kicking practice.

Month 4 (Weeks 14-17)

What's happening developmentally:

  • Rolling may begin (tummy to back usually comes first)
  • Purposeful grasping is reliable
  • Bringing objects to mouth (major exploration method)
  • Laughing out loud
  • Recognizes familiar people and may show stranger wariness
  • Can hold head steady without support
  • Sleep patterns may shift (4-month regression is common)

Appropriate activities:

  • Rolling encouragement: Place toys slightly to the side during tummy time to motivate turning.
  • Object exploration: Offer safe objects of different textures, temperatures, and weights. Baby will mouth everything — this is normal and important.
  • Supported standing/bouncing: Hold baby upright and let them bear weight on legs. Most babies love this.
  • Social games: Peekaboo, pat-a-cake, "so big!" — structured, repetitive games with anticipation.
  • Music and instruments: Shakers, soft drums, xylophones. Baby may start to bang or shake intentionally.
  • Water play: Splashing in a shallow basin (supervised). Great sensory input.

Month 5 (Weeks 18-21)

What's happening developmentally:

  • Sitting with support is stable; may sit briefly unsupported
  • Transferring objects hand to hand
  • Babbling emerges (consonant-vowel combos like "ba" "ma" "da")
  • Increasing mobility — rolling both ways, pivoting on tummy
  • Shows interest in food (signs of readiness for solids, though many wait until 6 months)

Appropriate activities:

  • Sit-and-play: Supported sitting with toys within reach. Great for core strength and hand-eye coordination.
  • Babble conversations: Mirror baby's babbling back to them. Add real words too — "Did you say 'ba'? Ball! Here's a ball."
  • Texture boards: Create boards with different textures (sandpaper, velvet, bubble wrap) for tactile exploration.
  • Cause and effect toys: Push-button toys, pop-up toys, anything where baby's action creates a result.
  • Outdoor exploration: Let baby touch grass, leaves, flowers (supervised). Novel sensory input.
  • Container play: Putting objects in and taking them out of a container. Early problem-solving.

Month 6 (Weeks 22-26)

What's happening developmentally:

  • Sitting independently (or nearly)
  • Introduction to solid foods (if not already started)
  • Understanding of object permanence beginning
  • Responding to own name
  • May start to creep or scoot
  • Separation anxiety may emerge
  • Pincer grasp developing (thumb and finger)

Appropriate activities:

  • Self-feeding exploration: Soft finger foods, spoons, cups. Messy but important for motor skills and independence.
  • Peekaboo (advanced): Hide toys under blankets for baby to find. Builds object permanence.
  • Crawling encouragement: Create a motivating environment — interesting toys just out of reach, tunnels, cushion obstacles.
  • Stacking and nesting: Cups, blocks, rings. Baby will mostly knock them down — that's the point.
  • Name games: Use baby's name frequently. Point to and name body parts, people, objects.
  • Music and movement: Action songs (Head, Shoulders, Knees and Toes), dancing, rhythm instruments.
  • Book interaction: Let baby turn board book pages, point to pictures, babble along.

Months 7-9 (Weeks 27-39)

What's happening developmentally:

  • Crawling (many variations are normal — army crawl, scooting, classic crawl)
  • Pulling to stand
  • Pincer grasp refining
  • Babbling with more variation and intentionality
  • Understands some words ("no," "mama," "dada," "bye-bye")
  • Stranger anxiety may peak
  • May start waving, clapping, pointing

Appropriate activities:

  • Obstacle courses: Cushions, tunnels, safe climbing surfaces for crawling babies
  • Finger foods and self-feeding: Increasingly complex — small pieces, different textures
  • Simple puzzles: Shape sorters, ring stackers, chunky knob puzzles
  • Imitation games: Clap and baby claps, wave and baby waves. Build on this for early social learning.
  • Treasure baskets: Collections of safe household objects for exploration (wooden spoon, fabric scraps, metal cup)
  • Water play: Pouring, splashing, floating toys in the bath or a basin
  • Social play: Playdates, park visits, interactions with other babies and children

Months 10-12 (Weeks 40-52)

What's happening developmentally:

  • Cruising (walking while holding furniture) and possibly first steps
  • First words may emerge
  • Understands simple instructions ("give me the ball," "where's daddy?")
  • Points to communicate
  • Emerging independence and will
  • More complex play behaviors (pretend feeding a doll, "talking" on a phone)

Appropriate activities:

  • Walking support: Push toys, walking with hands held, furniture arranged for cruising
  • Language-rich activities: Naming everything, simple questions, reading with pointing and naming
  • Art exploration: Large crayons, finger paint, playdough (supervised)
  • Building and knocking down: Block towers, stacking cups — baby may start building as well as destroying
  • Pretend play: Toy phones, dolls, kitchen items. Model pretend actions.
  • Outdoor adventure: Walking on different surfaces (grass, sand, pebbles), exploring nature
  • Sorting and matching: Simple sorting by size or color (with help)
  • Music making: More intentional — banging drums in rhythm, shaking maracas to songs

General Principles Across All Ages

Follow the baby's lead. If they're interested in something, lean into it. If they're not, try something else or try again later.

Wakeful windows matter. Activities should happen during alert, content periods — not when baby is hungry, tired, or overstimulated. Newborns may only have 30-45 minute wake windows; by 6 months it's 2-3 hours.

Repetition is not boring — it's learning. Babies love doing the same thing over and over. This is how neural pathways strengthen. Don't feel pressured to constantly introduce new things.

All five senses are learning channels. Visual, auditory, tactile, vestibular (movement/balance), and proprioceptive (body awareness). A good activity often engages multiple senses.

Talk, narrate, sing. Language exposure in the first year has an outsized impact on later language development. It doesn't need to be fancy — narrate diaper changes, describe what you're cooking, sing in the shower.

Outdoor time counts. Natural light, fresh air, and the rich sensory environment of the outdoors are genuinely beneficial for development (and parent mental health).

You are your baby's favorite toy. No product, gadget, or app comes close to the developmental benefit of engaged interaction with a loving caregiver.
